This article argues that chasing AI benchmarks is counterproductive for individual users. The key insight: the only honest and useful benchmark is your own real-world tasks and constraints. External leaderboards encode someone else's priorities, not yours.

Key takeaways:

## The Problem with Benchmarks
- Most new AI models feel incremental, overhyped, and interchangeable in day-to-day use
- New releases arrive in a flood—too fast to meaningfully test each one
- Many releases marketed as revolutionary are actually small tweaks (1.1–2.3% gains)
- Gap between perceived and actual change keeps growing

## Why External Benchmarks Are Flawed
- **Lab conditions vs real-world**: Benchmarks test narrow, artificial tasks, not your messy inbox, codebase, or documents
- **Leaderboard-optimized models often generalize poorly**: Tuned to narrow tasks rather than complex user scenarios
- **Miss real use cases**: Public benchmarks miss many tasks people actually use AI for
- **Encode others' priorities**: Someone else's data, judge, and preferences

## The Solution: You Are the Benchmark
Author started the "RBYF" (Revised Benchmark with You as a Feedback) project on GitHub.

**Core principle**: "Better on average" does not mean "better for you."

A model that tops leaderboards can still:
- Hallucinate on your niche domain
- Struggle with your language mix
- Break your existing stack

A carefully selected Small Language Model might:
- Integrate cleanly with your data
- Match your risk tolerance
- Fit your budget
- Be the real upgrade for your daily work

## How to Design Your Own Benchmark

**1. Define your real tasks/skills**: summarization, Q&A, learning, coding, etc.
- You likely don't need a 400B parameter model

**2. Track concrete outcomes**:
- Time saved on a task
- Error rate on real documents
- Number of revisions needed
- Team satisfaction (if applicable)

**3. Run small, honest experiments**:
- Same task, different tools, same constraints
- Record what felt reliable to YOU

**4. Use qualitative scoring** (example 0-5 scale):
- 0: Gibberish, made-up information
- 1: Bad quality, user intent not considered
- 2: Sufficient, barely coherent
- 3: Acceptable, some mistakes
- 4: Average, min mistakes, accurate
- 5: Perfect, compliant format, accurate

## Practical Advice
- **Use public benchmarks as a filter, not a verdict**: They tell you what to try, not what to trust
- **Follow papers, not just announcements**: ArXiv papers reveal meaningful progress in data pipelines, evaluation, inference optimization
- **If something feels off, trust your instinct**: Your metric may be missing from public ones

## Conclusion
"State of the art" should not be a number on a leaderboard—it should be when a model reliably makes your real life easier.

Your constraints, habits, and goals redefine what "state of the art" actually looks like. Don't outsource your judgment to external metrics.
