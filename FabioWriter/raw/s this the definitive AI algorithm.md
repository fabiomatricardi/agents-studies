A group of researchers may have found the best learning algorithm for AI, solving one of the biggest unsolved mysteries in AI training:

> **_How do we make use of rich feedback?_**

This article is aimed at people who want to intuitively understand how AIs are trained without the unnecessary jargon, maths, or formulas, **just first principles,** while still being textbook accurate, and also learning about a **new technique** that is going to revolutionize how it's going to be done going forward.

If that’s you, this article is for you.

## We learn what we can verify… and we’re bad at it.

AIs can only learn from verifiable signals. This is as true as saying birds can fly.

_But what does that even mean?_

### Imitation is the quintessential training method

Without feedback, without a way to verify whether they are progressing, they don’t know how to improve, just as humans would struggle considerably to learn about the real world without senses.

Machines aren’t as lucky as us (for now), so we have resorted to letting them imitate us to learn.

> If they can’t experience world feedback, we let them imitate the beings that do.

And for years, **most AI training was pure imitation**. We gathered a certain amount of human data, data built on millions of years of evolution and, in the case of Large Language Models (LLMs), thousands of years of writing, and we gave this data to AIs to imitate.

The earning process is straightforward at heart: the model predicts what it “believes” is the right response, and we compare it to the actual, ground-truth response, which serves as a learning signal.

> In the case of LLMs, for the sequence “What’s the capital of Egypt?”, the model assigns probabilities to every possible option, including 23% to ‘Cairo’. That ‘gap’ between 23% a 100%, which is what the model should have given Cairo, is the prediction error, so over time the model assigns very high probabilities to the correct predictions.

Via this rote repetition and a lot of, and I mean a lot, of it, **AIs eventually become reasonable human imitators of the area of application** (in the case of LLMs, natural language).

And while imitation has its obvious disadvantages (there are superior forms of learning, as we’re about to see), it offered a crucial advantage: **feedback density**.

It matters not only that you provide feedback for AIs to learn from, **but also the frequency of that feedback**. The higher the feedback frequency, the faster models learn.

But current frontier models like GPT-5.4 or Claude Opus 4.6, the types of models you use in your daily life, have an extra learning phase: **reinforcement learning**.

Imitation gives you an LLM that can perform very reasonable predictions. For the same example, _“What’s the capital of Egypt?”,_ the model is almost certain to provide a city as the response, not an animal. It may not accurately identify “Cairo” as the response and instead say “Alexandria”, but the response will not be “Capibara”.

That’s all well and good, **but imitation only takes you so far**. It’s reasonably good at compressing knowledge; the model does learn a lot of facts, **but it’s not great for problem-solving**, in the same way that dictation may help teach kids to write, but won’t take you very far in teaching them maths.

**For problem-solving tasks, we need exploration**. We need the model to trial-and-error its way into the answer. It needs to explore ways to solve the task to truly grasp the underlying concepts, just as kids only truly learn maths when they actually try the problem by themselves.

And leaves us to the other big training method: **exploration**.

### The dawn of exploration

For those limitations, LLMs are posteriorly trained using what we call Reinforcement Learning (RL), **which is just glorified trial-and-error learning**.

The model is not given the entire solution to copy, just the problem. Then, via continued exploration, trying and failing, and more trying and failing, **the model eventually reaches the correct response, which is then ‘reinforced’**; we incentivize the model to repeat the steps that took it to the right answer.

However, RL requires three very important components:

1.  **Intuition:** We can’t use a model without previous world knowledge for this. Otherwise, exploration becomes random guessing, which never converges (e.g., you can’t teach a dog to write Shakespeare poems, no matter how much the dog tries). This is why RL is always done after an imitation training phase.
2.  **Verifiability:** We need a way to validate the quality of a given outcome.
3.  **Rewards:** We need ways to ‘guide’ the model toward the correct response (i.e., we need models to have some sense of where they are heading). If you don’t give your dog treats after doing successful tricks, they will never learn that those particular tricks are desirable.

But done well, **one could consider RL the “definitive” training regime**. You are guaranteed to achieve results, but only if the three factors above are met.

-   The first one explains why we use LLMs and not dogs as subjects in the exploration phase, because they can make “educated guesses.”
-   The second one guarantees a learning signal; **without feedback, there’s simply no learning.**
-   The third one is perhaps the trickiest. Rewards are fundamental because they not only indicate how fast models learn, but also whether they learn good priors or become ‘misaligned’.

> Have you read recently about AIs blackmailing users? Well, **the reason this happens is poor rewards**, an issue called **reward hacking**. But we’ll leave that for another day.

Knowing all this, you can immediately understand why, done well, RL is so powerful. If the guesses are good enough, it’s a matter of time before the model guesses correctly, and “all” we need to do is push it to do the ‘things that worked’ more.

Thus, **you will be least surprised to learn that RL is the culprit of some of the most extraordinary achievements in AI’s history**: it’s behind AlphaGo’s superhuman Go capabilities, and it’s behind most of AI’s latest achievements in maths, coding, and agents.

But it has a huge problem that has, in a way, curtailed RL’s true powers until today: **reward quality and density.**

_And how has this research we are about to cover solved these limitations?_

## Making RL Densely Rewarded

When I say that RL has sparse rewards, I mean it. The model might generate 100 predictions in a blind state, unaware of whether it’s heading in the right direction, before it receives a reward that ‘hints’ at whether the approach is good.

This is a very inefficient trial-and-error approach, **because each trial yields very little information about its quality**. It’s like playing the game ‘hot or cold’ where the player gets yelled ‘hot’ or ‘cold’ if they get closer or further away from the object they have to find, **but only receives a signal once a minute, instead of every time they move.**

_But why just not give it all the rewards it needs?_ Well, it turns out, the real world is much more complicated.

### The credit assignment problem

The problem isn't that we don’t want to provide denser feedback; it’s that it’s very hard to do so.

**This is called the credit assignment problem**: for many predictions in the rollout (the entire set of predictions from the AI receiving the problem to generating a solution), **we don’t really know whether the prediction was good or not**.

It’s like writing a 5,000-word essay, and wondering whether writing ‘the’ as the 2,000th word is a good or bad choice to make in our quest to generate a brilliant essay. _Because_ _who knows?_

Some rewards are obvious; reinforcing a model’s behavior whenever it starts a novel with ‘Once upon a time,’ instead of with the word ‘Cattywampus’, makes sense; but thinks like ‘what is the perfect novel length’, ‘how to craft good side stories’ and other essential components of a good novel are far more complicated to verify as ‘good’ or ‘bad’.

And to make matters worse, in many cases, during AI training, the outcome reward is pretty mediocre, too.

For example, the model might generate an extremely long, thousands-of-words rollout to solve a complicated maths problem, **only to get feedback in the form of “yes” or “no”.**

_Is this really good feedback? Does it make it more likely that the next rollout is better?_

As the feedback we give models is most often mediocre, in most cases, **this exploration becomes a matter of statistical coverage**; the model will eventually stumble into the response “because statistics”, **not because it was effectively guided toward better solutions**.

And worse, sometimes the feedback is indeed very rich, **but we ignore it and just evaluate whether it was right or wrong**, leaving out the rest of the signal (i.e., feedback on how correct or wrong the model was).

_Why?_ Well, ironically, **because we don’t really know what to do with it** (more on this in a second).

Thus, we have the worst of both worlds: we have subpar reward frequency (the model is “blind” most of the time), and the outcome reward either doesn’t really say much beyond being correct or wrong, or we simply ignore that rich signal.

Luckily, **we might have found the answer**. It’s called Reinforcement Learning from Rich Feedback, or RLRF. But to understand what this is such a revolutionary idea, we first need to learn how models are trained with RL today.

### The problem with the status quo

There are two ways we know how to apply RL, differing in how the reward is applied: one uses an additional model, called a critic, while the other does not.

Having a more powerful model acting as a critic is great, but it is way more expensive. For that reason, the most popular public method for RL training today is GRPO, Group-Relative Policy Optimization, [created by DeepSeek](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2402.03300), the famous Chinese Lab, which doesn’t use a critic at all.

With all the fancy naming, **the method is actually quite straightforward:** we decide which rollouts are better based on relative scores.

In plain English:

1.  You have a problem and a scalar solution (the actual answer). You generate multiple rollouts with the model,
2.  score them individually,
3.  and then use those scores to weight the amount of learning the model earns from each rollout (i.e., the better the score from a rollout, the more it learns from that rollout).

This is cheaper than having another model acting as a critic, but you can also see the problems this technique has, **mainly reward density**.

We generate an entire rollout and receive only one reward: the outcome reward, which indicates whether the rollout was correct or incorrect. This raises two problems:

-   _In the rollouts that end in correct responses, which predictions in the hundreds that the rollout had were the key ones that led to the correct response?_ **Just because the final response was correct does not mean the model's reasoning was sound**.
-   _In the rollouts that ended in bad responses, were there any predictions in the hundreds that the rollouts actually had that were good?_ **Just because the final response was wrong doesn’t mean the model’s reasoning was bad.**

In other words, **this method has a serious supervision problem**: both the model and the engineers are blind to what is being learned; we are **guaranteed to be downweighting good predictions and reinforcing bad ones** (just because they turn out to be right).

Being clearer, under this method, a beautiful reasoning trace that makes a stupid mistake at the end gets downvoted, and a simple trace to the question _‘what is the circumference of a circle with radius 5cm’_ being _“the answer is π\*r²=31.42 cm”,_ **would be upvoted because the final answer is correct despite the formula being totally wrong.**

Therefore, to sum it all up, the perfect learning method woudl be one that:

1.  Has high reward frequency (the model is consistently made aware about how it’s doing ),
2.  Can use rich feedback beyond than just yesses and noes,
3.  It has that high reward density without being too expensive (e.g., requiring a much smarter and bigger model to critique the student)

And surprisingly, the answer to this problem might have been under our noses all this time. Let me explain how SDPO meets all those constraints.

### SDPO, A Super Exciting New Method

I always say the best research is the one that you immediately know is a great idea just by reading it. [This is one of such cases](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.20802).

As we’ve seen, what we were doing until now, having the AIs guess and verify at scale, eventually stumbling upon solutions that match the exact value they were supposed to, and reinforcing them, sort of worked.

I mean, the models you use in your daily life were trained exactly this way.

Interestingly, they have achieved this with rather mediocre feedback, despite the fact that, in cases like coding, the reward systems offer far more than just a ‘yes’ or a ‘no’; they provide information on what went wrong (compiler issues, broken specs, etc.).

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:512/0*Xbm8ilknpagULWN5)
As you can see in the final line, the environment clearly indicates what went wrong (we divided a number by zero). Source

However, we usually completely discard that information and instead tell the AI that the answer was just wrong.

You can immediately detect the issue; just as we don’t give a student a zero if the problem is okay-ish, **the idea here is to find a way to be able to provide the model the entire error feedback** (that it divided a number by zero)**, and naturally guide it toward better solutions**.

The problem here might not be obvious, but think about it: what score should we give to such feedback? How do we represent that precise mistake using numbers, which is the only thing machines understand?

Which is to say, how we go from the left in the image below, to the right?

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*Eo6FdvBtTGHWC9cyCIUOvg.png)
Source

> Well, as with all great research, the answer is kind of obvious: have the model retry with the new context.

In SPDO, [what researchers propose is the following](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.20802):

1.  We generate a rollout (i.e., the AI tries to solve the problem)
2.  That rollout generates rich feedback, giving insight into what went wrong (or right).
3.  However, and here’s the key, instead of discarding the rich feedback and just focusing on whether the AI got the right response, we take that feedback, send it back to the same model, and repeat
4.  The same model, but with the enhanced context, scores the entire rollout, acting as a critic

_But what is the advantage here?_ The key is that, while it’s the same model, it has better context, meaning the rollout can be scored based on something the previous model did not know.

> In other words, with the received feedback, we can use the model “as its own critic.” You might not have realized yet, but this changes everything.

_But why is this so revolutionary?_ The problem with rich feedback wasn't that we didn’t want it; **it was that we didn't know what to do with it**.

Scalar rewards are simple to use because they are numbers, numbers that can be easily compared to the ground-truth number, and thus easily interpreted mathematically.

But for feedback such as: _“Please answer in Newton-seconds”, how do we score that? Is this a 7 out of 10, an 8? Or a 2/10?_

Here, the researchers apply an ingenious trick: instead of treating it as a calculable reward, **just use it as additional context**. In plain English, using the new context, we run the model again on the same rollout.

In other words, it’s the exact same rollout, but the probabilities the model assigns to each prediction in it will change with the new context, because, and here’s the key, **now it has better context**.

This is the same as trying to solve a problem; someone gives you further context, and then you reevaluate every bit of your try but in a new light (i.e., “now that I have this additional context, I like what I did here, but I don’t quite like what I did there”).

> This is why they call their method SDPO (Self-Distillation Policy Optimization): **they use the same model to distill knowledge into itself**.

Crucially, unlike previous methods, **this method provides dense supervision**, meaning the teacher (the critic) evaluates every single prediction the student makes.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:654/1*BDclbFkBfjAd9H8ac5QI1Q.png)
In blue, the tokens the teacher highlighted as good. In red, the bad ones. Instead, GRPO is all-or-nothing, everything gets approved or rejected.

Suddenly, we have turned a process that, while still key to developing greater _“model intelligence”_, was expensive because it required other very expensive models or extremely long trial-and-error phases, because the rewards, what leads to actual improvements, were too sparse, into something that is:

1.  **Cheaper.** Unlike critic-student methods that have to use expensive smarter models to act as critics, you now have to run the model two times per rollout, **but it’s the same model** (not a stronger one) and in a more parallelizable workload (more on this below)
2.  **Denser.** We turn a sparse-reward system that rejected feedback for not being usable into one that not only benefits from this rich feedback but also provides supervision at every single prediction, not just at the end of the rollout (i.e., the all-or-nothing approach).

_And what about in situations where the feedback is simply not rich at all? Is this useless in those instances? Does this method stop working?_

Not at all. Here, although less ideal of course, **whenever a rollout is successful** (got to the right answer), **it’s then immediately used as feedback in other generations**.

At the end of the day, the idea is always the same: compare a model with poor context with one that, while identical, has great context, be that provided by rich environment feedback or by other successful attempts.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*57ddM7dRHxBSX4YGzX8o6w.jpeg)

At this point, you’re probably still inclined to push back on the cheaper side of things; this feels cheaper than using a smarter and thus more expensive model, acting as a critic, but it still requires more computational effort than GRPO (no critic at all).

The key to why this is not true is two-fold: **parallelization**, **response efficiency**, and **faster convergence**.

I want to reinforce a point that is hard to grasp if you’re not an AI nerd like I am: **running an LLM is much cheaper if the model knows what it’s predicting**.

During inference (e.g., generating a try at a problem), the model does not know what the next prediction will be.

In such a case, each new word you predict is required to predict the one coming next. You can’t predict the entire sequence at once; **it’s inherently sequential and takes much longer**.

However, another story is when the model “knows” what it will predict. For example, during imitation learning, we know what we want the model to imitate, so we just give it the entire sequence and see what probability it assigns to each word, all in a single prediction.

This is hard to visualize, so I’ve built a visualization that clarifies this point; all 11 words are predicted simultaneously:

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:449/1*p7s0mMO4KVsWocvxT8Ofig.gif)

As you can guess, this is much faster, and it’s exactly the same way we run the model once we have rich feedback. We know what the model we’re training generated; **we are just evaluating what that same model scores each word now that it has better context**.

This is not only faster **but also achieves greater hardware utilization** (i.e., better GPU utilization), resulting in a process that is only marginally slower than GRPO because the verification process was so fast.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:374/1*Khfp6a-wOVRT-vJACl_d6g.png)
Source

To further strengthen the ‘SDPO is cheaper’ argument, **it also leads to more efficient model responses**. As the feedback they get is much denser, they ‘yap’ way less and get to the point faster.

They don’t have to generate unnecessarily long generations to find the actual answer; instead, **they can use the clear feedback they receive to converge faster**.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*pODxplKb5Up-zTp2GUGbyg.png)

So… _is this it?_

## Is SDPO it?

For all that I’ve seen on this paper, it’s very likely that this becomes the new preferred training method; **cheaper and better**, _what’s not to like about that?_

Furthermore, the verification method is GPU-pilled, because it uses a natively parallelizable verification, something that often goes under the radar amongst enthusiasts but is crucial for AI Labs spending billions on compute.

It’s also a first in the use of richer feedback from environments, **for once ending the tyrannical grip sparse verifiable rewards had in this industry**, which led to never-ending training runs, hoping the model runs into the key priors fast; now, Labs can focus on designing environments that provide rich feedback that is immediately adopted.

To me, **this research ticks all the boxes of what makes research great:**

-   **incentives-aware** (it improves research on areas researchers care about),
-   **reads as common sense** (how is this not supposed to work?),
-   and **relevant** to the current research interest: RL.

I rarely get impressed by research lately; everything feels the same, which is why some of you may have noticed I write less about research. **But once I read this paper, I knew this was it.**
