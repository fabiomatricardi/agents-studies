### The Argument That Sounds Right, but Isn’t Enough

I came across a LinkedIn post recently that [argued most leadership problems are not structural, they are human](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ricsteward_most-leadership-problems-are-not-structural-activity-7453752487582400512-w3CK). It pointed to familiar patterns. People respond to attention before instruction. They trust stories more than data. They avoid loss. They decide emotionally. The conclusion was simple. Effective leaders design with human nature.

There is truth in that. The problem is not that the argument is wrong. It is that it is incomplete in a way that leads to poor decisions, misallocated capital, and weakened governance. For boards and executive teams, that distinction matters more than it might first appear.

---

### A Familiar Boardroom Moment

A few years ago, I sat in on a board discussion about a transformation program that was clearly underperforming. Deadlines were slipping, costs were rising, and internal friction was increasing.

Management’s diagnosis was confident and consistent. There was resistance to change. There was a need for stronger alignment. Leaders needed to communicate more effectively.

The proposed response followed naturally. More town halls. More leadership engagement. More emphasis on culture and trust.

It all sounded reasonable.

What went largely unexamined was the system underneath. Decision rights were unclear across business units. Incentives rewarded local optimization over enterprise outcomes. Information was fragmented and arrived too late to inform decisions. Accountability for trade-offs was diffuse.

In that system, what was being described as resistance was, in many cases, a rational response. The organization did not have a human problem. It had a design problem. And yet, capital continued to be deployed into fixing behaviour.

---

### Why “Human Problems” Are So Appealing

I see leaders are advised to gravitate toward behavioural explanations. They are intuitive, relatable, and actionable in the short term. In other words, they’re kind of easy to solve.

Anyone who has worked at the board or executive level has seen decisions influenced by emotion, narratives shaping alignment, and individuals reacting to perceived loss. These patterns are well documented in ideas like [Prospect Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prospect_theory) and [Dual Process Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_process_theory).

But these are tendencies, not governing laws. More importantly, they show up differently depending on the system people are operating in. When we label problems as human, we risk treating outputs as causes.

---

## Behaviour Is an Output of the System

From a management science perspective, behaviour emerges from the system, it does not sit outside of it.

Organisations are shaped by decision rights, incentive structures, information flows, governance processes, and the cadence of decision-making. Within those conditions, behaviour takes form.

People do not resist direction in the abstract. They resist when authority is unclear or when they are held accountable without control. They do not automatically prefer stories over data. They rely on narrative when data is incomplete or contested. They do not universally avoid loss. They respond to how risk is framed and who carries the consequences.

What I see that gets labeled as human nature is often a patterned response to structural conditions.

---

### Complicated Problems vs Complex Systems

Part of the confusion comes from treating all leadership challenges as if they are the same type of problem.

Some are complicated. They can be addressed with expertise, better analysis, and clearer information.

Others are complex. They evolve as you intervene. Cause and effect are not linear. Outcomes emerge from interaction.

In complex environments, which is where most boards now operate, behavioural tweaks are not enough. You are not dealing with isolated decisions. You are dealing with systems that adapt to your actions. That requires a different level of thinking.

---

## The Risk of Oversimplifying Behaviour

The LinkedIn argument leans heavily on behavioural insights. That is not the issue. The issue is what disappears when behaviour becomes the primary explanation.

-   Incentives fade into the background.
    
-   Power dynamics are overlooked.
    
-   Governance mechanisms are underexamined.
    
-   Feedback loops and path dependence are ignored.
    

This is a form of reductionism. It simplifies the system to the point where the most important levers are no longer visible.

The result is predictable. Leaders invest in communication, alignment sessions, and engagement initiatives, while leaving decision architecture, incentives, and information systems largely untouched. Capital and resources flow to symptoms, not causes.

---

### What System Intelligence Actually Looks Like

The post suggests that designing with human nature is system intelligence. That is only part of the picture.

Real system intelligence, grounded in [Complex Adaptive Systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_adaptive_system) and [Systems Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systems_theory), requires understanding how the system behaves over time.

It means paying attention to feedback loops, delays, nonlinear effects, and the interaction between local decisions and system-wide consequences.

This is where second-order effects start to matter. Recognition programs can increase motivation, but they can also intensify internal competition if performance criteria are unclear. Narrative alignment can help people move faster, but it can also suppress dissent and reduce decision quality if alternative views are not protected. Empathy can improve relationships, but without accountability, it can weaken performance discipline.

Designing with behaviour in mind matters. It just is not enough on its own.

---

### The Cost Shows Up in Capital Allocation

For boards, this is not theoretical.

When leadership problems are framed as human, capital tends to be deployed into leadership development programs, culture initiatives, and communication efforts. These can be useful, but when structural issues remain, the return on that capital is limited.

The organization may become more self-aware or more aligned on the surface, but not materially more effective. In some cases, the interventions reinforce the behaviours they were meant to address.

This is where governance and capital allocation intersect. If the diagnosis is off, the investment will be too.

---

### A Brief Moment of Honesty

There is a reason this pattern persists. Redesigning systems is difficult work.

It involves challenging power structures, redefining roles, rethinking incentives, and changing long-standing processes. These are not neutral moves. They are often politically sensitive, personally uncomfortable, and met with a great deal of organizational resistance.

Focusing on behaviour is quicker and more acceptable. I have gone down that path myself. It is not about capability. It is about how demanding system-level work really is.

---

### A Better Question for Boards and Executives

The original post ends by asking where we are designing against human behaviour.

A more useful question might be this:

> **What patterns of behaviour is our system reliably producing, and what structural conditions are creating those patterns?**

That shift changes the conversation. It directs attention to where decision rights are unclear, how incentives are shaping behaviour, what information is missing or delayed, and which feedback loops are reinforcing the wrong outcomes.

It also forces a more disciplined look at capital allocation. Where are we investing to change behaviour instead of redesigning the system? Which initiatives are treating symptoms rather than causes? What would we stop funding if we took a system view seriously?

---

### Closing Thought

The idea that leadership problems are human is appealing because it feels practical and immediate. And it is not wrong.

But in complex organisational systems, it is incomplete in ways that matter.

If you want to improve outcomes, not just interactions, you have to look upstream. You have to examine and redesign the system that is producing the behaviour you are seeing.

Anything less is a more polished way of managing the consequences.

---

### Call to Action

If you are sitting on a board or leading an executive team, take one current issue you are facing and push it one level deeper.

-   Instead of asking what people are doing wrong, ask what the system is producing.
    
-   Instead of funding another behavioural intervention, pause and examine the structure that is shaping those behaviours.
    

You may find that the highest return on capital is not in changing people, but in changing the conditions under which they operate. If this resonates, let’s chat.
