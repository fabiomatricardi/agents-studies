### **TLDR**

-   Tech company illegally fired a QA worker after replacing him with an LLM, then offering a 40% pay cut as a “reassignment.”
-   Cost-saving AI is now not legal grounds for termination in China.
-   **_Reasoning: AI adoption is a “voluntary move to stay competitive” so the company cannot “shift the risks of advancing technology onto its employees._****_”_**
-   US equivalent: the WARN Act, written in 1988, no AI clause. The big bipartisan response is a Hawley-Warner bill that asks companies to _report_ AI layoffs to the DoL. **_Reporting. Not stopping._**

## The Lawsuit

A guy named Zhou worked at a Hangzhou tech company. **_QA supervisor._** 25,000 yuan a month. His job was **_babysitting a large language model_**: matching user queries to outputs, filtering illegal content, making sure the thing didn’t hallucinate something that would get the company in trouble.

In 2025 the company decided the LLM could babysit itself. They told Zhou to take a different role at 15,000 yuan, or leave. He refused. They fired him. They offered 311,695 yuan as severance, slapped on the standard “organizational restructuring” sticker, and assumed that was the end of it.

> It was not the end of it.
> 
> Zhou went to labor arbitration and won.

The company sued. Lost in the Yuhang district court.

Appealed to the Hangzhou Intermediate People’s Court. Lost again.

The appellate ruling came down at the end of April 2026, the second Chinese court decision in six months that says

> you cannot fire a worker because the model got cheaper. Offsetting the risk of technical advancement to employees is illegal.

### The Legal Hinge: “Major Change in Objective Circumstances”

China’s Labor Contract Law, Article 40(3), lets an employer terminate a contract when “a major change in the objective circumstances” makes the original deal impossible to perform. This is the escape hatch every Chinese HR department reaches for. It’s how you justify cuts after a merger, a relocation, a force majeure event.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*bTCuQidMoLwwaO9WQIiRkw.png)
https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/prc-legal-update-termination-of-7234382

The company argued AI adoption was that kind of change. The court said no.

Specifically, the intermediate court ruled that adopting an LLM was a “voluntary move to stay competitive” by the company.

> Voluntary.
> 
> Not force majeure.
> 
> Not a market collapse.

A choice. And because it was a choice, the risk of that choice belongs to the employer, not the employee. The court’s words, not mine: the company “shifted the risks of advancing technology onto its employees.” That’s the line that should be tattooed on a wall somewhere in the Bay Area.

The 40% pay cut wasn’t a reasonable reassignment proposal either. It was a constructive dismissal dressed up as an internal mobility opportunity.

A court drew a distinction between _the world changing under you_ and _you changing the world under your worker_. One is grounds for termination. The other is your problem to absorb.

I really want american law-makers to pay attention to this one. We hear so much propaganda about China and their communist system, the world is moving fast. The so called third world countries have a latest firmware of laws. They have younger lawmakers and actual willingness to understand and take action accordingly.

### What This Actually Means in Practice

If you’re a Chinese employer and an AI tool can now do the job your employee was hired for, here is your option set:

1.  Find the employee a comparable role at comparable pay
2.  Negotiate a mutually agreed exit with real money on the table
3.  Eat the cost of paying someone whose old job is automated

What is no longer on the menu: “We have an LLM now, here’s a small severance, security will walk you out.”

This is a structural cap on the AI-driven productivity arbitrage that has powered every “we shrunk headcount by 40% with AI” press release of the last two years. It doesn’t ban automation. It bans the specific move of using automation as the legal justification for firing someone to capture the cost difference as margin.

Beautiful, right?

## Why China Did This and Why It Matters

China is also in the AI race. Hard. They have their own labs, their own foundation models, their own DeepSeek-flavored cost-collapse story. This isn’t a country trying to slow down automation.

> The official line from courts and state media is essentially stating that no matter what the technology should advance, _and_ labor stability should be preserved, _and_ somebody other than the worker should pay the transition cost. The difference here is that who is somebody paying the cost in Chinese economy vs US economy. You know the answer.

You can be cynical about Chinese labor law. Plenty of Foxconn-net stories on the record. The party uses social stability as a stick when it suits, ignores it when it doesn’t.

Fine. Fair. None of that makes the legal principle in this ruling less interesting. The principle, distilled: corporate AI gains are not allowed to land entirely on the worker’s resume. In policy terms, that’s risk-sharing. The employer keeps the productivity. The employer also keeps the worker, or pays meaningfully to part ways.

This is how it should work IMO. Right?

## Now Compare With the United States

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*ReKks_vt7OufEEQXVwYzyw.png)

The US has the WARN Act, passed in 1988. It requires 60 days notice before **_mass layoffs, but only at companies with 100+ employees, and only if you’re cutting 50+ workers at a single site_**. Below those thresholds, no notice required. No notice, no transition support, no legal review of _why_ the cut happened.

There is nothing in WARN about AI. There is nothing in WARN about replacement. Just like our old dying lawmakers our legal system has nothing that has kept up with the needs of the people. There lies the irony. If we want to be at the top of the world about AI, we must provide the rest of the guidance too. Why cant we produce the technology as well as the guidance that works for everything? Something to think about…

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/0*UChpbvt7uQrjf8w3.jpg)
https://www.aicerts.ai/news/labor-protests-rise-amid-ai-job-security-fears/

A few examples of what happens when a law does not change and the companies start working around it .. IBM has spent decades perfecting the art of staying under WARN thresholds. Layoffs in waves of a few thousand at a time, scattered across sites, paired with NDAs and “early retirement” sweeteners. Same outcome, no public filing.

Klarna’s CEO bragged in May about shrinking headcount by roughly 40%, “in part because of AI.”

Salesforce dumped 4,000 customer support roles in September on the explicit logic that AI now does 50% of the work.

Duolingo announced it would stop using contractors for work that AI can handle. The contractors found out from a press release.

There is just nothing to protect workers from AI. Has anyone thought through this process? How does it end when there are no white collar jobs and unemployment grows to 15%?

USA has a few bills floating in the ether but none has the teeth to say “you can’t do it.” Not “you owe the worker a comparable role.”

Sorry, but no. This is a reporting requirement for a phenomenon a court in Hangzhou just told employers they cannot legally do. That is truly a protection.

## “Cost-Saving” Is Doing Heavy Lifting in That Sentence

The Chinese ruling targets a specific motive. **Cost savings.**

The court hinged on AI adoption being a “voluntary move” by the company, undertaken to capture margin, with the worker absorbing the downside. I can imagine american capitalist tech bros and their bootlickers losing their minds over it. CEOs preparing their “we care only about the value to our shareholders”… well if your shareholder is also your employee then what?

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/0*VOYu2z54Vx9yHkMy)
https://www.linkedin.com/posts/fortune_as-debate-continues-over-ais-true-impact-activity-7430733070368079873-4DNf/

Sam Altman of all people said it out loud last year: companies are “AI washing” their layoffs, blaming AI for cuts they wanted to make for cost reasons that have nothing to do with the model’s actual capability.

That’s the gap a US version of this ruling would have to close. China closed it by reading “voluntary competitive move” as employer-borne risk. America’s instinct, in our current configuration, would be to write a definition so narrow that any halfway-competent CFO could route around it in a weekend. That is it. There is nothing more to say about the priorities of the US government and the industry.

## Imagine a Concrete Scenario

This has happened before so although the situation is imaginary, the evidence exits. Now, Imagine a 200-person American SaaS company. They roll out an LLM-powered support agent. It handles 60% of tickets without human intervention.

> Management does the math: we can let go of 30 of our 50 support reps and keep margins stable.

In USA we write the severance checks, file no WARN notice (under 50 cuts, single site, exempt), and announce on the next earnings call that “AI productivity initiatives” reduced operational costs by X%. Stock pops. Done.

In China, those 30 reps now have to be offered comparable roles, or comparable pay in a different role, or a meaningful negotiated exit. The company can still automate. It just cannot pocket the wage savings as the entire ROI of the project.

See, you cant screw over someone just because you can. There is an implicit relationship between the employer and employee. China understands that. People build lives around where they get the job, the schedule, their kids go to schools in the same locality etc. Profit motive >> Firing cant just force people to uproot everything that they built in 60–90 days. No displacement without comparable replacement should be the norm.

Same technology. Same productivity gain. Two completely different distributions of who pays the human cost.

## The Part Nobody on the US Side Wants to Say

Workers replaced by AI in America right now have approximately the legal protection of an open browser tab.

-   The federal posture is: track the damage and publish a report.
-   The Chinese posture is: you cannot do the thing.

One is post-mortem of what was done, other one is saying STOP.

This is my perspective. You should do what you are comfortable with.

The math doesn’t lie. China just told its tech sector that AI productivity comes with a labor bill the company has to pay. The US just asked its tech sector to please send in a form when convenient..

But if you’re a knowledge worker in the US watching the AI-washing layoff cycle hit your function, the honest read is: there is no Zhou case coming to rescue you. I hope you are ready to defend your job and life.
