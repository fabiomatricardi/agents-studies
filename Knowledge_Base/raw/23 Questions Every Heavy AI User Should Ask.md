## 4. Atul Gawande (1965 - ) 

Seneca’s most famous line is ”It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a lot of it.” He did not suffer from the laziness that afflicts most of us. 

He was one of the sharpest minds in Rome—second in charge to Nero while he was boy—and he had a nightly practice where he reviewed his entire day. “What did I sa What did I do? What could I have done better?” He describes it in _De Ira_ (on anger) _,_ and he makes it sound remarkably undramatic: he doesn’t frame it as self-punishme or spiritual discipline or personal development but as standard maintenance. 

He already knew how to live well (although for what we know from his writings on happiness, he could’ve done a better job). The review was about making sure he wa doing it. Seneca understood something that I think applies directly to us: knowing what to do and _actually doing it_ are different problems. 

Fifteen centuries later, Ignatius of Loyola—a Spaniard, which I appreciate—took this idea and turned it into a system: _The Examen_ . Five steps and five minutes. Twice a d 

It was designed for Jesuits, the most committed, most disciplined practitioners in th Catholic world, so he tailored it, naturally, for religious stuff. But it’s a useful referen because it was _for experts_ . (You may not think of yourself as such, but consider what 99% of the population knows about using AI and then think again.) 

Loyola understood that the review becomes more valuable the more competent yo already are—not the other way around—because 1) complacency hides in compete and 2) an incompetent person could not even parse it! A beginner may not know w they don’t know, but an expert forgets how much they know. 

Then Ben Franklin—probably the one that’s most familiar to you—took the whole thing out of the spiritual domain—this is during the 18th-century Enlightenment—a made it practical with his 13 virtues. He tracked one per week, cycled through all 13 every quarter, and kept a chart (he would have loved Claude Excel). 

Franklin’s system made explicit what Seneca and Loyola left implicit: any single day’ answers are meaningless; “I failed at Temperance today, so what?” The insight is _in t pattern across months_ . He also said, and I love this, that he never achieved the perfection the system aimed at, but that _the practice of checking_ made him better th he would have been without it. 

Finally, Atul Gawande—the only one here that’s still alive—proved all of this empirically in 2009. 

He wrote _The Checklist Manifesto_ after studying a case at Johns Hopkins: a team created a five-step checklist for inserting central line catheters. When they made it mandatory to run the checklist every time for ICU doctors—who knew it by heart—t infection rate dropped from 11% to zero! 

Essentially, they stopped skipping steps they had been skipping solely motivated by the _tricky disability of expertise_ . Gawande’s key finding is that the doctors who resist the checklist the most were the most senior ones. It felt beneath them. And yet, the were the ones who benefited the most. That’s us, you and me, pioneers in the use o AI, paving the way for those who come after, failing to notice our flaws. 

Summing up: Seneca introduced the principle of the checklist, Loyola focused it on experts, Franklin introduced consistency, and Gawande proved it empirically.

This maps directly onto how we’re using AI. 

And I’m going to show you how. 

As I see it, we are in the Seneca stage, trying to develop some principles and good practices as we go. This is good, but also primitive and heuristic-based. 

Nobody (that I’m aware of) does a monthly AI retrospective. 

Or checks whether their dependency has deepened, their skills have atrophied, thei taste has drifted toward the model’s defaults, or whether they’re producing more b thinking less. We just keep going. There are papers and surveys and studies every n and then that talk about this (MIT, Wharton, etc.), and the occasional blog post, but you want to wait for that? I don’t think so. 

This will eventually be an imperative task: Every field where the stakes are high enou eventually builds some kind of periodic review into the practice. Surgery, aviation, spiritual life, personal development, and now AI. This technology is already reshapin how millions of people work, and it has zero review culture around it. 

I think we need a checklist. 

So I made one. 

It’s 23 questions. 

This is a prototype, a personal checklist I’ve done for myself, to keep me on track. T broad idea is to make the user reflect on the use of AI and improve it from the very of asking these questions (also from the answer, of course). These questions will evolve, change, and I will add new ones, but it’s a starting point that I think can be useful for you as well. 

Steal it and run it once a month. Even a quick skim is better than nothing! 

Eventually, you will want to iterate on these questions as you see fit. This is not intended to be a rigid, fixed, one-fits-all checklist, but the exact opposite: ideally, yo will have a checklist so tailored for your work and use cases that, by the time it is tru useful to you, none of my questions will remain. 

Take this post as my attempt to get you going!

## Your work 

1. Did you edit or rework every output before shipping it? 

2. Did you catch the AI getting something wrong this month? 

3. Was your vibe-coding worth the time you saved quality-wise? 

4. If you timed your AI-assisted tasks end-to-end, did you save time? 

5. Are you better at your job than you were a month ago? 

## Your skills 

6. Did you do at least one task without AI this month that you normally use AI for 

7. Could you still do your most important task at the same quality without AI? 

8. Did you sit alone with a difficult problem before reaching for AI? 

9. Did you develop an original idea this month that AI inspired? 

10. Can you tell when AI output is wrong in your domain? 

## Your growth 

11. Did you make something you could not have made without AI? 

12. Did you hold back from producing something despite it being easy to make? 

13. Is your AI-assisted work better than your pre-AI work? 

14. Did you learn to do something novel with the help of AI? 

## Your habits 

15. Did you replace AI´s default structure with your own at least once? 

16. Did you tailor your prompts to each tool? 

17. Have you used at least once everything you built with AI? 

18. Did you use the right tools for the right tasks? 

19. Did you double-check an output that confirmed your biases? 

## Your dependency 

20. Could you do tomorrow’s work without any AI? 

21. Do you use every AI tool you’re currently paying for? 

22. If the price doubled, could you walk away without panic? 

23. Could you switch to a competing AI without rebuilding everything?
